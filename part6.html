üü£ 1. Italian Cuisine

Key Dishes

Pizza (flatbread topped with tomato sauce, cheese and other toppings, baked in an oven)

Pasta (noodles such as spaghetti or penne, served with sauces like tomato or pesto)

Gelato (rich and creamy Italian-style ice cream)

Characteristics

Uses simple, fresh ingredients such as tomatoes, olive oil, basil and garlic.
Focuses on strong yet clean flavours instead of too many mixed spices.
Many dishes highlight local cheeses, cured meats and seasonal vegetables.

Cultural Significance

Meals are a time for family to sit together, talk and relax slowly.
Local regions in Italy are proud of their own special pastas, cheeses and sauces.
Sharing pizza or pasta with friends is a common way of celebrating everyday life.

üü£ 2. Thai Cuisine

Key Dishes

Pad Thai (stir-fried rice noodles with eggs, tofu or meat, peanuts and lime)

Green curry (coconut-based curry with green chilli, vegetables and meat)

Som tam (spicy green papaya salad with lime, chilli and peanuts)

Characteristics

Balances four main tastes in one meal: sweet, sour, salty and spicy.
Coconut milk, fish sauce, lemongrass and lime leaves are common ingredients.
Lots of herbs and fresh vegetables are used to keep dishes colourful and fragrant.

Cultural Significance

Street food stalls are an important part of Thai daily life, especially in busy cities.
Food is usually served in many small dishes to share at the centre of the table.
Eating together builds friendship and respect within families and communities.

üü£ 3. Ethiopian Cuisine

Key Dishes

Injera (large sour flatbread used as both plate and utensil)

Doro wat (spicy chicken stew cooked with berbere spice and boiled eggs)

Misir wat (lentil stew flavoured with spices and onions)

Characteristics

Meals are served on top of injera, and pieces of injera are used to scoop the food.
Berbere, a spice mix made from chilli and other spices, gives dishes a deep red colour.
Many dishes are plant-based, using lentils, peas and vegetables, especially on fasting days.

Cultural Significance

Sharing food from one large injera tray symbolises unity and friendship.
Feeding someone by hand, called ‚Äúgursha‚Äù, is a sign of love and respect.
Meals are often accompanied by a traditional coffee ceremony that brings people together.

üü£ 4. Lebanese Cuisine

Key Dishes

Hummus (smooth dip made from chickpeas, tahini, lemon and garlic)

Falafel (deep-fried balls made from ground chickpeas or beans)

Shawarma (sliced, seasoned meat served in flatbread with salad and sauces)

Characteristics

Uses olive oil, lemon juice, garlic and herbs like parsley and mint.
Meals often begin with many small dishes, called ‚Äúmezze‚Äù, placed on the table.
Flatbreads such as pita are used to scoop dips and wrap fillings.

Cultural Significance

Food is central to hospitality; guests are offered generous plates of mezze and tea.
Large family gatherings feature long tables filled with colourful dishes.
Sharing food is a way to show generosity and welcome to visitors and neighbours.

üü£ 5. American Street Food

Key Dishes

Hot dogs (sausages in a bread roll, topped with mustard, ketchup or onions)

Burgers (minced meat patties in a bun with cheese and salad)

Food truck specials like tacos, fries, fried chicken and pretzels

Characteristics

Strong influence from many immigrant communities (Italian, Mexican, Asian, African).
Portions are often large, with bold flavours and many sauces.
Fast and convenient, designed to be eaten on the go during busy days.

Cultural Significance

Street food and food trucks are common at festivals, sports games and city corners.
BBQs and grilling in the backyard are popular for family and national celebrations.
Certain foods, like hot dogs at baseball games, have become part of national traditions.

üü£ 6. South African Cuisine

Key Dishes

Braai (barbecue of meat cooked over open flames)

Bunny chow (hollowed-out bread loaf filled with curry)

Bobotie (baked minced meat dish with spices and egg topping)

Characteristics

Mixes African, Dutch, Indian and Malay influences in one cuisine.
Dishes often use maize, beans, meat and chutneys for sweet-savoury flavour.
Spices are used warmly, but not always extremely hot, creating comfort foods.

Cultural Significance

Braai is more than just cooking; it is a social event bringing neighbours and friends together.
Food traditions vary by region and community, reflecting South Africa‚Äôs diversity.
Sharing a meal is an important way of breaking barriers and building unity.

üü£ 7. Kenyan Cuisine

Key Dishes

Ugali (firm maize meal porridge eaten with stews and vegetables)

Nyama choma (grilled meat, usually goat or beef, eaten with kachumbari salad)

Chapati (soft flatbread usually served with beans or meat stew)

Characteristics

Staple foods include maize, beans, sukuma wiki (kale) and potatoes.
Flavours are often mild to medium, using onions, tomatoes and curry powder.
Different communities have special dishes, such as pilau at the coast or mursik in Rift Valley.

Cultural Significance

Eating ugali together from one plate symbolises sharing and unity in the family.
Nyama choma joints are popular gathering places to watch football and talk with friends.
Food is a key part of weddings, harambees and holiday celebrations across Kenya.

üü£ 8. Greek Cuisine

Key Dishes

Moussaka (layered dish with eggplant, minced meat and white sauce)

Souvlaki (skewered and grilled meat served in flatbread or on plates)

Greek salad (tomatoes, cucumbers, olives and feta cheese with olive oil)

Characteristics

Uses olive oil in almost every dish, along with herbs like oregano and thyme.
Fresh vegetables, fish and cheeses are central to everyday meals.
Food is often simple but full of flavour, relying on good ingredients rather than heavy sauces.

Cultural Significance

Meals are long and relaxed, encouraging conversation and storytelling.
Many dishes have roots in ancient traditions and local village cooking.
Eating outdoors in courtyards or by the sea is common during warm weather.

üü£ 9. Korean Cuisine

Key Dishes

Kimchi (fermented cabbage or vegetables seasoned with chilli and garlic)

Bibimbap (rice bowl topped with vegetables, meat and egg, mixed with chilli paste)

Korean BBQ (grilled meat cooked at the table and wrapped in lettuce leaves)

Characteristics

Meals include many side dishes called ‚Äúbanchan‚Äù, placed in small bowls.
Flavours are spicy, salty and umami, often using fermented pastes and sauces.
Rice is the main staple, accompanied by soups, stews and pickled vegetables.

Cultural Significance

Sharing meat at a Korean BBQ restaurant is a popular way to spend time with friends.
Kimchi is eaten almost daily and is considered essential to Korean identity.
Traditional meals reflect respect for elders, with polite table manners and seating order.

üü£ 10. Moroccan Cuisine

Key Dishes

Tagine (slow-cooked stew made in a cone-shaped pot, with meat and dried fruits)

Couscous (tiny steamed grains served with vegetables and broth)

Mint tea (sweet green tea flavoured with fresh mint leaves)

Characteristics

Combines sweet and savoury tastes using dried apricots, dates, almonds and spices.
Common spices include cumin, cinnamon, turmeric and saffron.
Meals are often aromatic, colourful and served in large communal dishes.

Cultural Significance

Serving mint tea to guests is a strong symbol of hospitality and friendship.
Families gather around low tables, sharing from one big dish of couscous or tagine.
Food traditions are connected to markets, desert caravans and ancient trading routes.

üü£ 11. Indonesian Cuisine

Street food markets, called ‚Äúwarungs‚Äù, are important places for cheap and tasty meals.
Food shows the mix of many islands and cultures across the Indonesian archipelago.
Meals are usually eaten with family, and sharing from large platters is common.
